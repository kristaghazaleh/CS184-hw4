Introduction and Overview

In this project we implement a real-time cloth simulation based on a mass-spring system and then extend that system into a visually rich cloth rendering pipeline. We begin by discretizing the cloth into a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs, which gives the sheet a simple but effective physical model for stretching, skewing, and resistance to folding. We then simulate motion by accumulating external and internal spring forces, advancing the masses with Verlet integration, and enforcing stretch limits to maintain stability. After the core dynamics are in place, we add collisions against spheres and planes, incorporate self-collision using spatial hashing so that the cloth can fall onto itself efficiently, and finally render the result with a sequence of GLSL shaders.

Across the assignment, the cloth evolves from a regular wireframe grid into a more physically plausible animated surface that can sag, drape, wrap around rigid bodies, and settle into layered folds. Each stage of the project adds a different piece of realism. The spring network defines the local mechanical behavior of the cloth, numerical integration determines how that behavior unfolds in time, collision handling keeps the sheet from passing through external objects, and self-collision prevents different regions of the cloth from occupying the same space. The shader portion then translates this simulated geometry into different visual appearances, ranging from diffuse and Blinn-Phong shading to texture mapping, bump mapping, displacement mapping, mirror reflection, and a custom water-like surface effect.

One of the most interesting aspects of the project is how closely the physical and visual components depend on one another. Small changes in density, spring stiffness, or damping produce noticeably different drape patterns, and those geometric differences become even more apparent once lighting and shading are applied. In the same way, shader-based surface detail can dramatically change the way the cloth reads visually even when the underlying simulated mesh is unchanged. Working through the full pipeline reinforced that believable cloth behavior is not produced by a single technique, but by the interaction of geometric discretization, force modeling, stable time integration, collision response, and shading.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

Point-Mass Grid Construction and Coordinate Layout

In Part 1, the cloth is discretized into a regular lattice of point masses spanning the physical cloth dimensions. Let \(w\) denote the cloth width, \(h\) denote the cloth height, \(N_w\) denote the number of point masses along the width direction, and \(N_h\) denote the number of point masses along the height direction. The implementation of Cloth::buildGrid begins by clearing any existing masses and springs, then constructing all \(N_w N_h\) point masses before inserting any springs. Because the masses are sampled uniformly over the sheet, the spacing between adjacent masses is

\[ \Delta x = \frac{w}{N_w - 1}, \qquad \Delta y = \frac{h}{N_h - 1}. \]

These spacings define the cloth's rest configuration, so the spring rest lengths are inherited directly from the geometry of the initial grid. If the grid is indexed by integers \((x,y)\) with \(0 \le x < N_w\) and \(0 \le y < N_h\), then a horizontal cloth lies on a plane of constant \(y\)-coordinate and varies over the \(xz\)-plane, while a vertical cloth varies over the \(xy\)-plane. For a horizontal sheet, the initial position is

\[ \mathbf{p}_{x,y}^{\text{horizontal}} = (x \Delta x,\; 1,\; y \Delta y). \]

For a vertical sheet, a small random offset \( \varepsilon \in [-10^{-3},\,10^{-3}] \) is added in the \(z\)-direction to prevent the cloth from starting in an exactly coplanar, perfectly symmetric state. In that case, the initial position is

\[ \mathbf{p}_{x,y}^{\text{vertical}} = (x \Delta x,\; y \Delta y,\; \varepsilon). \]

Pinned masses are determined from the scene's pinned coordinate list. Pinning changes the boundary conditions of the sheet without changing the resolution of the lattice itself, so two scenes can have the same \(50 \times 50\) point-mass grid while still deforming differently because different subsets of masses are fixed in place.

Row-Major Storage, Pinning, and Indexing

The point masses are stored in row-major order, meaning the implementation iterates across each row and then advances to the next row. This ordering is required because later code, especially buildClothMesh(), assumes that the two-dimensional grid has been flattened into the one-dimensional point_masses vector using the index map

\[ I(x,y) = y N_w + x. \]

Here, \(I(x,y)\) is the one-dimensional storage location of the point mass at grid coordinate \((x,y)\). If the storage order were inconsistent with this map, later neighbor lookups and triangle construction would attach the wrong vertices. The pinning logic is conceptually separate from the geometry: every point mass is inserted at its regular lattice position, but each mass is tagged as either movable or pinned. A pinned mass therefore remains part of the cloth connectivity while acting as a fixed boundary condition during later simulation steps.

Spring Topology: Structural, Shearing, and Bending Constraints

After all point masses have been constructed, the second pass through the grid inserts the three required spring families: structural, shearing, and bending. Delaying spring construction until after the point_masses vector is complete is important because each spring stores pointers to its endpoint masses. If springs were inserted while the vector was still growing, reallocation could invalidate previously stored pointers.

Structural springs connect each mass to its immediate left neighbor and immediate upper neighbor. These springs are the primary resistance to stretching and compression along the horizontal and vertical lattice directions, so their rest lengths are one grid step, namely \( \Delta x \) or \( \Delta y \) depending on orientation within the grid.

Shearing springs connect each mass to its upper-left and upper-right diagonal neighbors. Their purpose is to resist in-plane skewing of the local grid cells. If \( \Delta x \) and \( \Delta y \) are the horizontal and vertical grid spacings, then the diagonal rest length of a shearing spring is

\[ \ell_{\text{shear}} = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}. \]

Bending springs connect each mass to the mass two steps to the left and two steps above. These springs act over a larger neighborhood than the structural springs, so they do not mainly preserve local edge lengths. Instead, they resist sharper local changes in curvature and suppress overly abrupt folding. Their rest lengths are two grid steps in the corresponding direction. Each spring is added only once by connecting a point mass to previously visited neighbors such as left, above, upper-left, upper-right, two-left, and two-above. This avoids duplicate springs while still covering the full constraint graph of the cloth.

Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Force Accumulation, Hooke's Law, and Verlet Integration

In Part 2, the cloth begins to behave as a dynamic system rather than a static spring network. The simulation step starts by computing the mass associated with each point mass from the total cloth mass. Let \(w\) and \(h\) denote the cloth dimensions, let \(\rho\) denote the cloth density, and let \(N_w\) and \(N_h\) denote the numbers of point masses along the width and height directions. Then the mass \(m\) assigned to a single point mass is

\[ m = \frac{w h \rho}{N_w N_h}. \]

External accelerations, such as gravity, are converted into forces by multiplying each acceleration vector by the point-mass mass \(m\) and summing over all prescribed external accelerations. If \(\mathbf{a}_{\mathrm{ext}}\) denotes an external acceleration and \(\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{ext}}\) denotes the resulting external force on a point mass, then \( \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{ext}} = m \mathbf{a}_{\mathrm{ext}} \). This total external force is assigned to each point mass at the beginning of the simulation step.

After the external forces are initialized, spring forces are accumulated using Hooke's law. Let \(\mathbf{x}_a\) and \(\mathbf{x}_b\) denote the current positions of the two spring endpoints, let \(l\) denote the spring's rest length, and let \(k_s\) denote the spring constant. Then the spring force is

\[ \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{spring}} = k_s \frac{\lVert \mathbf{x}_b - \mathbf{x}_a \rVert - l} {\lVert \mathbf{x}_b - \mathbf{x}_a \rVert} (\mathbf{x}_b - \mathbf{x}_a). \]

Equal and opposite spring forces are applied to the two endpoints so that the total internal force remains balanced. Structural and shearing springs use the full spring constant, while bending springs are weakened relative to the others so that the cloth resists large-scale folding without becoming unnaturally rigid.

Once the total force is known, the acceleration of a point mass is obtained from Newton's second law, \( \mathbf{a}_t = \mathbf{F}_t / m \), where \(\mathbf{F}_t\) is the total force at the current time step. The positions of all non-pinned masses are then advanced using Verlet integration. Let \(d\) denote the damping fraction and let \(\Delta t\) denote the simulation time step. If \(\mathbf{x}_t\) is the current position and \(\mathbf{x}_{t-\Delta t}\) is the previous position, then the update is

\[ \mathbf{x}_{t+\Delta t} = \mathbf{x}_t + (1-d)\bigl(\mathbf{x}_t - \mathbf{x}_{t-\Delta t}\bigr) + \mathbf{a}_t \Delta t^2. \]

This update preserves momentum implicitly through the difference \( \mathbf{x}_t - \mathbf{x}_{t-\Delta t} \), while the factor \(1-d\) gradually removes energy from the motion. Pinned masses are excluded from this position update so that they continue to act as fixed boundary conditions for the cloth.

Post-Integration Stretch Correction

After Verlet integration, some springs may have stretched too far during a single time step. To control this, the simulation applies the Provot-style stretch correction required by the assignment. Let \(l\) denote the rest length of a spring. The current spring length is required to satisfy

\[ \lVert \mathbf{x}_b - \mathbf{x}_a \rVert \le 1.1\,l. \]

If the spring is longer than \(1.1\,l\), the excess extension is projected onto the spring direction to form a correction vector. If neither endpoint is pinned, the correction is split evenly between the two masses. If exactly one endpoint is pinned, the entire correction is applied to the unpinned endpoint. If both endpoints are pinned, no positional correction is applied. This post-integration step prevents extreme stretching and helps stabilize the cloth, especially when the spring constant is low or when the cloth is undergoing large motion.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

Collision Handling in Cloth::simulate

In Part 3, collisions are incorporated after the Verlet integration step and before the spring stretch-limiting correction. Once the new tentative positions of the point masses have been computed, the simulator loops over every point mass and every collision object in the scene and asks each collision object to resolve contact with that point mass. This separates the global simulation logic from the object-specific collision geometry: the cloth simulator is responsible for advancing the masses in time, while each collision class is responsible for defining how its own surface corrects a penetrating point mass.

In our implementation, this means that the method Cloth::simulate iterates through the point-mass set and calls collide(pm) on each collision object. The actual geometric correction is therefore implemented inside Sphere::collide and Plane::collide, while the cloth class simply dispatches the point masses to the appropriate collision routines.

Sphere Collision Geometry and Correction

Let \( \mathbf{o} \) denote the center of the sphere, let \( R \) denote its radius, and let \( \mathbf{x} \) denote the current position of a point mass after integration. The sphere collision test first checks whether the point mass lies inside the sphere by measuring its distance from the center:

\[ \lVert \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{o} \rVert < R. \]

If this inequality is satisfied, the point mass has penetrated the sphere and must be moved back to the sphere surface. The outward radial direction from the sphere center to the point mass is

\[ \widehat{\mathbf{r}} = \frac{\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{o}}{\lVert \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{o} \rVert}. \]

Using this unit vector, the corresponding tangent point on the sphere surface is

\[ \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{tan}} = \mathbf{o} + R \widehat{\mathbf{r}}. \]

Rather than correcting from the current point-mass position directly, the implementation computes the correction from the previous position \( \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} \), which is stored as last_position. If the raw correction vector is

\[ \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{tan}} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}}, \]

and if \( f \in [0,1] \) denotes the sphere friction coefficient, then the corrected position is

\[ \mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} + (1-f)\mathbf{c}. \]

This moves the point mass back to the sphere surface while allowing friction to reduce the full correction. Geometrically, the result is that the cloth wraps over the sphere instead of passing through it, while still retaining some physically plausible sliding behavior along the surface.

Plane Collision Geometry and Correction

Let \( \mathbf{p} \) denote a point on the plane, let \( \mathbf{n} \) denote the plane normal, let \( \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} \) denote the previous point-mass position, and let \( \mathbf{x} \) denote the current position after integration. The signed distances of these two positions from the plane are

\[ s_{\mathrm{last}} = (\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} - \mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{n}, \qquad s_{\mathrm{curr}} = (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{n}. \]

A collision occurs when the point mass crosses from one side of the plane to the other during the time step, which is detected by a sign change in these signed distances. Once a crossing is detected, the segment from \( \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} \) to \( \mathbf{x} \) is intersected with the plane. If the segment is written as

\[ \mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} + t(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}}), \qquad 0 \le t \le 1, \]

then the tangent point on the plane is obtained by solving \( (\mathbf{x}(t) - \mathbf{p}) \cdot \mathbf{n} = 0 \), giving

\[ t = \frac{(\mathbf{p} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}})\cdot \mathbf{n}} {(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}})\cdot \mathbf{n}}, \qquad \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{tan}} = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} + t(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}}). \]

To prevent the cloth from numerically sinking into the plane, the corrected target is displaced slightly off the surface by a small surface offset \( \varepsilon \), in the direction of the side from which the point mass came. If \( f \) is the plane friction coefficient, the final corrected position again has the form

\[ \mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}} + (1-f)\bigl(\mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{target}} - \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{last}}\bigr). \]

This makes the cloth settle on the plane rather than clipping through it. The surface offset keeps the cloth numerically above the plane, while the friction factor prevents the correction from behaving like a perfectly elastic bounce.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

Spatial Hashing and Self-Collision Response

In Part 4, the cloth must prevent different regions of the sheet from occupying the same space. A naive self-collision method would compare every point mass against every other point mass, which would require \(O(n^2)\) pairwise distance checks for \(n\) masses. That quickly becomes too expensive for a dense cloth mesh. To reduce this cost, we use spatial hashing. The main idea is to partition space into buckets, store nearby point masses in the same bucket, and then only test self-collision against point masses that lie in the same bucket rather than against the entire cloth.

Let \(w\) and \(h\) denote the physical cloth width and height, let \(N_w\) and \(N_h\) denote the numbers of point masses along the width and height directions, and let \(\tau\) denote the cloth thickness. Following the assignment specification, the spatial map uses bucket dimensions

\[ w_s = \frac{3w}{N_w}, \qquad h_s = \frac{3h}{N_h}, \qquad d_s = \max(w_s, h_s). \]

Here, \(w_s\) is the bucket width, \(h_s\) is the bucket height, and \(d_s\) is the bucket depth. For a point mass at position \(\mathbf{x} = (x,y,z)\), the corresponding integer bucket coordinates are

\[ i = \left\lfloor \frac{x}{w_s} \right\rfloor, \qquad j = \left\lfloor \frac{y}{h_s} \right\rfloor, \qquad k = \left\lfloor \frac{z}{d_s} \right\rfloor. \]

These integer cell coordinates are combined into a hash key. The function hash_position computes that key, and build_spatial_map rebuilds the entire spatial map each simulation step by inserting a pointer to every point mass into the bucket associated with its current position. In other words, the map stores a relation of the form

\[ \text{hash key} \;\longmapsto\; \{\text{point masses contained in that spatial cell}\}. \]

After the map has been built, self_collide only inspects the point masses stored in the same bucket as the current point mass. Let \(\mathbf{x}_i\) denote the position of the point mass being corrected and let \(\mathbf{x}_j\) denote the position of another point mass in the same bucket. A self-collision response is triggered whenever the two point masses are closer than twice the cloth thickness:

\[ \lVert \mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j \rVert < 2\tau. \]

If this inequality holds, the two point masses overlap too closely and the current point mass must be pushed away from its neighbor. The pairwise correction vector points from \(\mathbf{x}_j\) to \(\mathbf{x}_i\) and has magnitude equal to the overlap distance:

\[ \Delta \mathbf{x}_{ij} = \frac{2\tau - \lVert \mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j \rVert} {\lVert \mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j \rVert} (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j). \]

The implementation skips the case \(i=j\) so that a point mass does not collide with itself, and it also ignores zero-distance cases to avoid dividing by zero. If there are \(m\) valid overlapping neighbors in the bucket and if \(s\) denotes the number of simulation substeps, then the final correction applied to the current point mass is the average of all pairwise corrections divided by \(s\):

\[ \Delta \mathbf{x}_i = \frac{1}{m\,s} \sum_{j=1}^{m} \Delta \mathbf{x}_{ij}. \]

This averaging prevents a point mass from being over-corrected by many nearby neighbors in a single time step, while the division by \(s\) distributes the repulsion more gently across the substeps. Algorithmically, the Part 4 update therefore proceeds in three stages: rebuild the spatial hash map from the current cloth configuration, query each point mass against the masses in its own bucket, and apply an averaged repulsion correction whenever self-overlap is detected. The result is a cloth that can fall, fold, and pile onto itself without obvious large-scale self-penetration.

Part 5: Shaders

What a shader program is and how the two stages work together

A shader program is a small GPU program that runs during rendering and decides how geometry is transformed and how pixels are colored. In this homework, the vertex shader is responsible for taking each mesh vertex from model space into clip space, while also passing forward the data that the fragment shader will need later, such as position, normal, tangent direction, and texture coordinates. If \( \mathbf{p} \) is a model-space point and \( M \) is the model transform, then the clip-space position used for rasterization has the form

\[ \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{clip}} = VP\,M\,\mathbf{p}, \]

where \(VP\) is the combined view-projection transform. Once the triangles are rasterized, the GPU interpolates the per-vertex data across the surface of each triangle. The fragment shader then uses this interpolated data to evaluate the lighting model or texture lookup at every pixel. That separation is what makes the full pipeline work. The vertex stage determines where the surface is and what geometric data is carried forward, while the fragment stage determines what the rendered surface looks like once light, reflection, and textures are applied.

This division is especially important in the later shaders. For example, bump mapping leaves the mesh itself unchanged and only modifies the normal used in the fragment stage, while displacement mapping actually changes the vertex positions before rasterization and then shades the displaced surface afterward. In other words, the vertex shader controls geometry and the fragment shader controls appearance, and the most convincing results come from using both stages together instead of relying on only one of them.

Blinn-Phong shading model

The Blinn-Phong model combines three lighting terms. The ambient term gives the surface a small base illumination even when it is not facing the light directly. The diffuse term models how strongly the surface faces the light source, so it becomes brighter when the normal aligns with the light direction and darker when it turns away. The specular term models the shiny highlight and depends on how well the normal aligns with the halfway direction between the light and the camera. The full model can be written as

\[ L = k_a I_a + k_d I_l \max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{l}) + k_s I_l \max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{h})^p, \]

where \( \mathbf{n} \) is the surface normal, \( \mathbf{l} \) is the unit light direction, \( \mathbf{v} \) is the view direction, and

\[ \mathbf{h} = \frac{\mathbf{l} + \mathbf{v}}{\lVert \mathbf{l} + \mathbf{v} \rVert} \]

is the halfway vector. We used the cloth for these renders because the folds make the differences between the terms easy to read. In all four collected Phong renders, the user interface still displayed Normal equal to 2 and Height equal to 0.1, but those controls are not part of the Phong shader itself, so the visual result here comes entirely from the ambient, diffuse, and specular lighting terms.

The ambient image is almost entirely a dark silhouette because the ambient coefficient is intentionally small. That is expected behavior, since ambient light is only meant to keep the surface from dropping completely to black. The diffuse image shows the smooth large-scale shape of the cloth because its intensity is controlled by \( \max(0,\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{l}) \). Folds that face the light become brighter and folds that turn away become darker. The specular image contains only a small set of bright highlights because the glossy term is concentrated where \( \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{h} \) is large. This is why the highlights appear as narrow bright streaks along the curved folds rather than as a broad change in brightness over the whole cloth.

Once the three terms are added together, the cloth looks much more complete. The ambient contribution keeps the dark side of the cloth readable, the diffuse term gives the folds their broad shape, and the specular term adds the bright glossy accents that make the material feel less flat. The final image therefore looks the way it does because each term contributes a different visual role rather than because any single term is doing all the work. This section was a good reminder that realistic shading usually comes from combining several simple terms that each encode one different part of the lighting behavior.

Texture mapping with a custom texture

For texture mapping, we used a custom image of Mercury sourced from NASA. The shader is mathematically simple: it interpolates the surface texture coordinates \( (u,v) \) across the triangle and samples the image by evaluating

\[ \mathbf{c}(u,v) = \mathrm{texture}(u\_texture\_1,\mathbf{uv}). \]

The sampled color is then written directly to the output fragment color. This means the visual result comes entirely from the UV map and the chosen image, not from any height-map or reflection computation. Since the source image is itself a circular planetary render, it looks best on the sphere. Mapping it onto the sphere reinforces the idea of a planet-like surface and avoids the awkward empty black corners that appear if the same image is stretched across a large flat sheet. The result is a clean example of how UV interpolation and texture lookup can project a detailed image onto a relatively simple mesh.

Bump mapping

For both bump mapping and displacement mapping, we deliberately chose a spiraled mosaic texture instead of the default texture. The image contains curved grout lines, cracked tile surfaces, and strong local contrast across several scales. That makes it an excellent height-map source, because the shader can interpret bright and dark changes as small surface variations instead of as only color changes. The spiral structure also produces an immediately readable directional pattern on both the sphere and the cloth, which makes the bump and displacement comparisons easier to understand.

In the bump shader, the mesh geometry itself is not moved. Instead, we sample nearby height values from the same texture and estimate the local slope by finite differences:

\[ dU = \bigl(h(u+\tfrac{1}{w},v)-h(u,v)\bigr)k_h k_n, \qquad dV = \bigl(h(u,v+\tfrac{1}{h})-h(u,v)\bigr)k_h k_n. \]

These derivatives define a local perturbed normal

\[ \mathbf{n}_{\mathrm{local}} = \frac{(-dU,-dV,1)}{\lVert (-dU,-dV,1)\rVert}, \]

which is then transformed into the surface frame through the tangent-bitangent-normal matrix. The result is that the lighting behaves as if the surface contains real tile relief, even though the actual silhouette has not changed. On the sphere, the pattern reads like a fine engraved shell. On the cloth, the same texture rides over the large cloth folds and creates a layered effect in which the simulated cloth shape and the mosaic microrelief are both visible at once.

Displacement mapping

Displacement mapping uses the same texture and the same normal-perturbation idea in the fragment stage, but it adds one critical geometric step in the vertex shader. If \( h(u,v) \) is the sampled height and \( \mathbf{n} \) is the model-space normal, then the displaced vertex position is

\[ \mathbf{p}' = \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{n}\,h(u,v)\,k_h. \]

This is the key difference between bump and displacement. In our final implementation, the fragment-stage lighting logic is shared, but displacement additionally offsets the vertices before rasterization, while bump leaves the vertex positions untouched. That is why displacement can change the silhouette and the underlying surface geometry, whereas bump only changes the way light reacts to the surface.

On the sphere, displacement produces a more physically convincing tile relief because the crack network is no longer only a shading illusion. On the cloth, the effect becomes even more dramatic because the displaced mosaic pattern rides on top of the larger cloth folds and creates visibly sharper creases and geometric variation. We used Normal equal to 100 and Height equal to 0.03 in these main displacement renders so that the geometry change would be obvious without causing the mesh to break apart.

Low-resolution sphere comparison with \( -o\ 16\ -a\ 16 \)

The low-resolution comparison highlights why mesh resolution matters. With only a coarse sphere available, bump mapping still produces visually rich local relief because it relies mainly on the fragment-stage normal perturbation rather than on actual new geometry. Displacement, by contrast, is limited by the small number of vertices available to move. Even though the same texture is used, the coarse sphere can only approximate the height field in a blockier way because there are fewer geometric samples across the surface. This makes the difference between the two methods smaller at low resolution than it is at high resolution.

High-resolution sphere comparison with \( -o\ 128\ -a\ 128 \)

At high resolution, the bump and displacement results separate more clearly. The bump shader remains visually similar because its detail is always produced through the perturbed normal field, which does not require extra mesh tessellation. The displacement shader, however, benefits directly from the much denser vertex grid. Since many more vertices are available, the geometry can follow the spiral mosaic texture more faithfully and the displaced surface becomes smoother and more believable. Looking across both the low-resolution and high-resolution comparisons, the overall lesson is that bump mapping is inexpensive and visually effective even on coarse meshes, while displacement mapping becomes significantly more convincing as the mesh gets finer because it needs geometric degrees of freedom in order to represent the height field.

Mirror shader

The mirror shader computes a reflection direction from the view ray and the surface normal and then samples the environment cubemap in that reflected direction. If \( \mathbf{i} \) is the incident view direction and \( \mathbf{n} \) is the unit surface normal, then the reflection vector is

\[ \mathbf{r} = \mathrm{reflect}(\mathbf{i},\mathbf{n}) = \mathbf{i} - 2(\mathbf{i}\cdot\mathbf{n})\mathbf{n}. \]

The fragment color is then taken from the cubemap as \( \mathrm{texture}(u\_texture\_cubemap,\mathbf{r}) \). On the sphere, the result is smooth and highly readable because the geometry is regular and the reflected environment is warped only by the sphere curvature. On the cloth, the same cubemap becomes stretched and folded by the draped geometry, which makes the reflective material look much more dynamic. This pair is a good example of how the same shader can look very different depending on the underlying shape of the mesh.

Custom ocean shader

For the custom shader, we wanted a stylized but still physically motivated ocean surface rather than a generic glossy material. The shader was inspired by Tessendorf-style ocean ideas, especially the use of directional waves, Fresnel-based reflectivity, and choppy crest formation. Instead of using only one repetitive sine wave, we combined several directional wave components with different frequencies, phases, and amplitudes so that the surface would not look like an evenly striped pattern. At the vertex stage, the wave field controls both vertical displacement and a smaller horizontal choppy displacement, which sharpens wave crests and broadens troughs.

If \( \omega_i \), \( \phi_i \), \( a_i \), and \( \mathbf{d}_i \) denote a set of wave frequencies, phases, amplitudes, and directions, then the height field follows the general form

\[ H(u,v) = \sum_i a_i \sin\!\bigl(\omega_i\,(\mathbf{d}_i\cdot(u,v)) + \phi_i\bigr), \]

and the surface normal is recovered from finite differences of that wave field. In the fragment stage, we mix reflected environment light with transmitted water color through a Fresnel-style reflectance term. In simplified form, the optical blend is

\[ C = R(\theta)\,C_{\mathrm{reflect}} + \bigl(1-R(\theta)\bigr)C_{\mathrm{water}} + C_{\mathrm{glint}}, \]

where \(R(\theta)\) increases at grazing angles. This makes the surface more reflective near shallow viewing angles and more water-colored when viewed more directly, which is one of the main visual cues that helps the result feel like water instead of polished plastic.

Several features were important for making the result convincing. First, we used more than one wave direction so the surface would not look mechanically repetitive. Second, we added choppy displacement so the waves would have sharper and more ocean-like ridges. Third, we kept the color palette in a narrow blue range so the plane reads as a continuous water body rather than a patterned fabric. Finally, we rendered the custom shader on the plane instead of on the sphere or draped cloth, because a broad flat surface makes the swell structure and the view-dependent reflectance much easier to read. If we wanted even more realism, the next improvements would be time-dependent phase evolution, a richer spectral wave construction, and additional amplitude envelopes to break up repetition further across the surface.
